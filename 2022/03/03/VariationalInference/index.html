Variational Inference

Posted on 2022-03-03 In Statistics

The goal of variational inference is to approximate the conditional probability of a latent variable \(x\) under the observed variable \(y\) (in the context of dimensionality reduction, our data). We choose a tractable distribution \(q(x; v)\), where \(v\) represents model hyperparameters, as an approximation to simulate the intractable true distribution \(p(x \mid y)\).

We can rewrite \(\log p(y)\) to decompose it into two terms. can be written as the sum of the following two terms:

\[ \begin{equation} \log p(y)=\underbrace{\int q(x) \log p(y, x) dx -\int q(x) \log q(x) d x}_{L(q(x)), E L O B(\text { Evidence Lower Bound })}+\underbrace{\int q(x) \log \frac{q(x)}{p(x \mid y)} d x}_{K L(q(x) \| p(x \mid y))}. \label{eq1} \end{equation} \]

The latter term is known as the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, which is a method of quantifying the difference between two distributions, and is either larger than or equal to zero at all times. It equals 0 if and only if the two distributions are identical .

Since \(\log p(y)\) is a fixed quantity for a given set of observations, maximizing the is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence, i.e., minimizing the distance between the two distributions.

This conclusion can also be obtained by use Jensen’s inequality to show that

\[ \begin{align} \log p(y) \geq ELOB. \label{eq2} \end{align} \]

Based on this fact, instead of trying to maximize the likelihood directly, we can try to maximize the lower bound of the likelihood and still can optimize the parameters as well as make predictions.

Proof of Equation \(\eqref{eq1}\), approach showing the relationship between marginal likelihood, ELOB, and KL divergence:

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \log p(y) &=\log p(y, x)-\log p(x \mid y) \\ &=\log \frac{p(y, x)}{q(x)}-\log \frac{p(x \mid y)}{q(x)} \\ &=\log p(y, x)-\log q(x)-\log \frac{p(x \mid y)}{q(x)} \\ &=\log p(y, x)-\log q(x)+\log \frac{q(x)}{p(x \mid y)} \end{aligned} \end{equation} \]

Integrate \(q(x)\) on both sides of the above equation

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \int q(x) \log p(y) d x &=\int q(x) \log p(y, x) d x-\int q(x) \log q(x) d x \\ &+\int q(x) \log \frac{q(x)}{p(x \mid y)} d x \end{aligned} \end{equation} \]

Since \(q(x)\) and \(p(y)\) are independent, \(\int q(x) \log p(y) dx = \log p(y)\), then

\[ \begin{align} \log p(y)=\underbrace{\int q(x) \log p(y, x) dx -\int q(x) \log q(x) d x}_{L(q(x)), E L O B(\text { Evidence Lower Bound })}+\underbrace{\int q(x) \log \frac{q(x)}{p(x \mid y)} d x}_{K L(q(x) \| p(x \mid y))}. \end{align} \]

Another approach using Jensen’s inequality (Equation \(\eqref{eq2}\)):

\[ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \log P(y) &=\log \left(\int P(y, x) d x\right) \\ &=\log \left(\int\left(\frac{P(y, x)}{q(x)} q(x)\right) d x\right) \\ &=\log E_{x q(x)}\left[\frac{P(y, x)}{q(x)}\right] \\ & \geq E_{x q(x)} \log \left(\frac{P(y, x)}{q(x)}\right) \\ &=E_{x q(x)} \log P(y, x)-E_{x q(x)} \log q(x) \\ &=\int q(x) \log P(y, x) d x-\int q(x) \log q(x) d x \end{aligned} \end{equation} \]
